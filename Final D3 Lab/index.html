Every Continent Is on Fire

2023 was the most catastrophic wildfire year on record. Canada alone lost 18 million hectares. This is more than any single country in recorded history. The Amazon suffered its worst drought in 50 years. Southern Europe saw villages evacuated as fires raged for weeks across Greece, Portugal, and Spain.

"We are not in a new normal.
We are in a new abnormal that will
keep getting worse."

The direct cause is climate change: higher temperatures dry out vegetation faster, fire seasons last longer, and when fires start they burn hotter and spread faster than firefighters can contain. Sub-Saharan Africa, often overlooked in Western media coverage, accounts for the largest share of global burned area by far.

Global Temperature Anomaly 1880 – 2023

The Temperature Spiral

Each arc of the spiral represents one year. The further a year's arc sits from the center, the warmer that year was compared to the pre-industrial baseline.

The innermost arcs(the late 1800s) barely deviate from the center. Through the mid-20th century, a slow creep outward begins. Then, after 1980, the spiral accelerates dramatically.

2023 broke through the 1.5°C threshold that scientists warned must not be crossed, making it the hottest year in 125,000 years of Earth history.

The Connection
Temperature Anomaly vs. Fire-Weather Days · by Country

Hotter Countries Burn Longer

Every dot is a country. The warmer a nation has become since 1880, the more fire-weather days it experiences each year. Dot size shows total hectares burned in 2023. The trend line reveals an almost linear relationship and its implications are alarming.
